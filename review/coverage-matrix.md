# Coverage Matrix — *Value Creation: An Applied Distillation of MBA & Marketing*

**Purpose.** Map the book's 21 chapters against a canonical MBA-core + marketing + entrepreneurship ontology, mark each topic **Covered / Partial / Absent**, and separate *intentional* omissions (consistent with the book's "value-creation spine" scope) from *accidental* gaps that would hurt a real entrepreneur.

**What this is NOT.** This measures **presence and breadth of coverage only** — not correctness, currency, or quality. Those need the separate source-truth audit + expert review. A "Covered" cell can still be wrong.

**Method.** Built from the book's *actual* content: 21 chapters, their section titles, 48 labelled equations, and 34 metric cards (ch.20). Ontology anchored to `research/curriculum-canon.md` (12 disciplines validated against HBS/Stanford GSB/Wharton/Booth/Kellogg/MIT/INSEAD/LBS syllabi) plus the standard MBA-core additions and startup-founder topics it omits.

**Legend.** ✓ Covered (dedicated, substantive) · ◐ Partial (touched, or one-chapter-thin, or sub-topics missing) · ✗ Absent.

## Verdict: intentional vs accidental gaps

The book's declared spine is **the sequence of firm-level decisions that create enterprise value**, explicitly *not* a full MBA. Judge completeness against that — but several absences both fit the spine *and* are missing, which makes them accidental.

**Defensibly intentional (outside the value-creation spine):**
- Macroeconomics · business law/IP · HR depth · global/international · services marketing · classical advertising/PR · business communication · exits.

**Accidental & consequential — these sit *on* the value-creation spine and an entrepreneur will hit them. Ranked by impact:**

1. **Accounting / financial-statement literacy** — the book runs DCF, NOPAT, FCF, margins without ever teaching how to read or build the statements they come from. Foundational; a reader can't actually execute ch.14–16 without it.
2. **Fundraising, cap tables, dilution, runway** — core to a founder's value-creation *and financing* decisions; the finance section stops at valuation.
3. **Startup financial modeling / operating projections** — the bridge between unit economics (06/19) and valuation (15) that founders actually build.
4. **Sales management & GTM motion** — the running example is B2B SaaS; demand-gen is covered but the sales engine that closes it isn't.
5. **Product management / NPD** — the company is a product; what to build is never addressed.
6. **Experiment design & inferential statistics** — the book relies on A/B tests and incrementality RCTs (10) but doesn't teach how to design or read them.
7. **Distribution / channels (the 4th P)** — partnerships, marketplaces, channel economics.
8. **Data & AI strategy + ESG/governance depth** — both flagged by the canon as urgent 2026 additions; both only glanced at.

**Depth flags (Covered-but-thin):** Operations (17) and Org Behavior (18) are single chapters standing in for whole disciplines — fine for a distillation, but a reviewer should down-weight "Covered" there to "Covered, introductory."

**One structural strength worth recording:** the book's distinctive contribution — cross-linking the *same* quantity across disciplines (the growing perpetuity = CLV = terminal value; ROIC>WACC = LTV:CAC) — is fully realized in coverage terms (48 cross-referenced equations, capstone integrates all parts). The thesis is *delivered*; the question for the expert/reader review is whether it's *correct* and *useful*.
