\chapter{Digital Marketing: Funnel, Lifecycle \& Growth Loops}\label{ch:digital-funnel-and-crm}

% Scope: the funnel as a probability chain; lead scoring & the CRM pipeline;
%   growth loops vs the flywheel. Sits before the platform mechanics of \cref{ch:paid-acquisition-platforms}.

Before you buy traffic you need the architecture that turns it into customers. The digital funnel is not a metaphor; it is a chain of conditional probabilities, and every downstream decision --- which platform, which bid, which audience (\cref{ch:paid-acquisition-platforms}) --- is an attempt to move one of its terms.

\section{The Funnel as a Probability Chain}\label{sec:funnel-chain}

\begin{decision}
Spend is wasted unless it survives every step from first impression to paid customer. Which step is the binding constraint --- and what is one more customer actually worth at the top?
\end{decision}

\begin{intuition}
Awareness, consideration, and conversion are not stages you ``move down''; they are gates, each with a pass-through probability. The whole funnel multiplies, so the smallest-probability gate dominates the outcome --- doubling a \qty{2}{\percent} step beats squeezing a \qty{40}{\percent} one. The idea that persuasion proceeds in measurable stages is the hierarchy-of-effects model \parencite{lavidge1961model,barry1987development}.
\end{intuition}

\begin{derivation}
Decompose the join from impression to sale by the chain rule of probability (\cref{ch:decisions-under-uncertainty}):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:funnel-chain}
  P(\text{sale}) \;=\; P(\text{click}\mid\text{impression})\,\cdot\,P(\text{lead}\mid\text{click})\,\cdot\,P(\text{sale}\mid\text{lead}).
\end{equation}
Each factor is one funnel gate; the product is the end-to-end conversion rate. Expected new customers are simply impressions $\times$ \cref{eq:funnel-chain}.
\end{derivation}

\begin{breaks}
The chain assumes the gates are independent and the path is linear. Neither holds exactly: a retargeted user re-enters mid-funnel, and a strong brand lifts every gate at once. Treat \cref{eq:funnel-chain} as the first-order model, not the territory --- multi-touch reality is the subject of \cref{ch:attribution-and-measurement}.
\end{breaks}

\begin{worked}
Our running subscription business sends \num{10000} sessions to a trial page. \qty{8}{\percent} start a trial (\num{800}); \qty{25}{\percent} of trials convert to paid (\num{200}). End-to-end that is a \qty{2}{\percent} session-to-customer rate. Lifting trial-start from \qty{8}{\percent} to \qty{12}{\percent} yields \num{300} customers --- a \qty{50}{\percent} gain --- whereas a same-effort bump in the already-healthy trial-to-paid gate moves far fewer.
\end{worked}

\begin{trap}
Optimising the loudest gate, not the binding one. Teams pour creative budget into top-of-funnel click-through while a broken trial-onboarding gate quietly halves the product. Find the lowest-probability, highest-traffic gate first.
\end{trap}

\section{Lead Scoring \& the CRM Pipeline}\label{sec:lead-scoring}

\begin{decision}
Not every lead deserves a salesperson's hour. Which leads get worked now, and what is the pipeline worth before any of them closes?
\end{decision}

\begin{intuition}
A CRM pipeline is the funnel made discrete: named opportunities sitting in stages, each with a historical close probability. Lead scoring ranks them by fit and behaviour (the MQL$\to$SQL handoff) so scarce sales effort meets the leads most likely to convert \parencite{jarvinen2016harnessing}. The same logic recurs across the customer lifecycle, not just at acquisition \parencite{lemon2016understanding,kumar2018customer}.
\end{intuition}

\begin{derivation}
Value the pipeline as a probability-weighted sum over open deals:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pipeline-ev}
  \mathbb{E}[\text{pipeline}] \;=\; \sum_{i} D_i\,p_i,
\end{equation}
where $D_i$ is deal size and $p_i$ the stage close-probability. Past behaviour is the strongest predictor of future value; recency, frequency, and monetary value (RFM) remain the workhorse scoring features \parencite{fader2005rfm,bult1995optimal}.
\end{derivation}

\begin{worked}
Forty opportunities average \money{1200} at a stage close-rate of \qty{30}{\percent}: \cref{eq:pipeline-ev} values the pipeline at $40\times\money{1200}\times0.30=\money{14400}$. Raising the MQL bar lifts the close-rate to \qty{45}{\percent} but admits only \num{28} deals: $28\times\money{1200}\times0.45=\money{15120}$ --- more expected revenue from less sales effort.
\end{worked}

\begin{trap}
Scoring on fit alone. A perfect-fit account that never opens an email is not sales-ready; behaviour gates intent. And feeding raw, unqualified leads back to ad platforms teaches them to find more unqualified leads (\cref{ch:paid-acquisition-platforms}) --- pass back the \emph{qualified} signal.
\end{trap}

\section{Growth Loops \& the Flywheel}\label{sec:growth-loops}

\begin{decision}
A funnel spends to fill itself and stops when spend stops. Can the output of acquisition fund its own next input?
\end{decision}

\begin{intuition}
A loop feeds back: customers produce something --- referrals, content, data --- that acquires the next customers. Where a funnel is linear and leaky, a loop compounds; Collins' flywheel is the same image of accumulating momentum \parencite{collins2001good,ellis2017hacking}.
\end{intuition}

\begin{derivation}
For a referral loop, define the coefficient
\begin{equation}\label{eq:viral-k}
  k \;=\; i \times c,
\end{equation}
the invites sent per user $i$ times the conversion per invite $c$. When $k>1$ each cohort more than replaces itself and growth is self-sustaining; when $k<1$ the loop amplifies paid acquisition but does not replace it \parencite{leskovec2007dynamics}.
\end{derivation}

\begin{deeper}
$k$ is the marketing twin of the network-effect multiplier in \cref{ch:growth}: both ask whether each user makes the next one cheaper or more likely. Loop throughput also obeys the same queue logic as \cref{ch:operations} --- cycle time between invite and conversion bounds how fast the loop spins.
\end{deeper}

\begin{contested}
Whether ``funnel'' or ``flywheel'' is the right primitive is genuinely disputed \parencite{meyer2019marketing,vazirani2023flywheel}. The honest synthesis: loops do not abolish the funnel, they re-feed its top --- you still need \cref{eq:funnel-chain} to convert the traffic a loop delivers.
\end{contested}

A funnel sized by \cref{eq:funnel-chain}, a pipeline valued by \cref{eq:pipeline-ev}, and a loop measured by \cref{eq:viral-k} are the three shapes of digital demand. Which traffic to buy for them, and how, is \cref{ch:paid-acquisition-platforms}. \textcite{kumar2018customer} develop the CRM and customer-equity side in full.
